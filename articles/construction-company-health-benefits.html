Health Benefits for Construction Companies: What Small Crews Can Actually Offer in 2026

By Marcus Reid  ·  The Benefits Desk  ·  Sun Mar 15 2026 20:00:00 GMT-0400 (Eastern Daylight Time)

The Labor Problem Construction Owners Know Too Well

Finding experienced tradespeople in 2026 is harder than at any point in the last decade. Framers, concrete finishers, project superintendents, foremen — the skilled labor market is tight and getting tighter.

When workers have options, they exercise them. And one of the most common reasons a qualified candidate picks the other company isn't pay rate. It's benefits.

The larger GC offers health coverage. You don't. That conversation ends badly more often than most construction owners want to acknowledge.

The assumption most small contractors carry: health benefits are for companies with 30 or 40 people. We're not there yet.

That assumption is outdated by about a decade.

What the Old Model Looked Like

Traditional group health insurance required critical mass. The employer selects a carrier, gets quoted on a group plan, and to qualify — typically 5 to 10 employees had to enroll, and 70% of eligible workers had to participate.

For a construction company with 8 to 15 employees, a rotating subcontractor base, and seasonal swings in headcount, this model was almost impossible to make work. Even when it did, the premium was fixed. You paid whether your crew used it or not. It went up every year. And the administrative burden fell entirely on you.

Most small contractors looked at that picture and said no. That's still the picture most owners think they're looking at.

It's not the only option anymore.

What Actually Changed

The market has shifted toward individual coverage. Instead of forcing a crew onto a single group plan, employers now have the option to give employees access to a marketplace of individual plans — and each employee works with a licensed professional who reviews their doctors, their prescriptions, and their budget before recommending anything.

No single blanket plan. No group negotiation. No participation minimum.

Some structures require no monthly contribution from the employer. Others let you set a fixed dollar amount per enrolled employee, so you control the total cost and only pay for the people who actually use it.

For a construction operator managing cash flow, project margins, and a variable crew, this is the model worth understanding.

Two Options Worth Knowing

Individual marketplace access. This is the newer model — and it's what most small contractors haven't seen yet. A platform built specifically for small businesses installs a benefits marketplace inside your company. Each employee connects with a licensed professional who walks them through available plans one on one, reviews their doctors, their prescriptions, and their budget, and helps them pick what actually fits their situation. The employer cost can be zero. You get to tell recruits and current employees that you offer benefits. Your people get real, professional guidance. The platform handles the rest. Most construction owners we talk to haven't heard of this model — which is exactly why it's worth paying attention to right now.

Level-funded plans. For construction companies with 10 or more employees who want a more structured, group-style benefit, self-funded plans are worth a conversation. You pay a fixed monthly amount — typically 20 to 40 percent less than a traditional fully-insured premium — and if your crew's healthcare usage comes in under projections, you may get money back at year end. A healthy, physically active crew in the trades often benefits from this model. It requires more administrative involvement than the individual marketplace approach, but the economics can work strongly in your favor.

Neither of these is the complicated, expensive group plan most owners picture when someone says "employee benefits."

What It Actually Costs

The number that stops most construction owners: what does this actually cost me?

At the access-only end, the employer cost can be zero. Employees who want coverage enroll, pay for their own plans with professional guidance, and you've provided a genuine benefit with no monthly overhead.

If you want to contribute — and many owners do, because a contribution is a recruiting tool — most small contractors in construction land between $100 and $300 per enrolled employee per month. On a full-time hourly basis, $200/month is about $1.15 per hour. For crew members deciding between your company and the next one, that's often the number that moves them.

You're also not paying for the person who doesn't enroll. That's the structural difference. Traditional group plans bill you for coverage whether it's used or not. Individual-based models bill you for participation.

The Signal It Sends

Benefits aren't only a financial decision. They're a signal about how you run your business.

When a foreman you want to hire asks about health coverage — and they almost always do — "we offer it" changes the conversation. You're no longer the small contractor that can't compete with the regional GC. You're the company that invests in its people.

Contractors who have added a benefits structure consistently report the same two things: better candidates in the door and better retention numbers once they're there. Not because the plans are extravagant. Because the signal changed.

What to Do Next

The options are more accessible than most construction owners realize. The question is which structure fits how you actually operate.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a construction company with part-time or seasonal workers offer health benefits?
Yes. Individual-based benefit structures don't require a minimum number of enrolled employees or a fixed participation rate. You can offer access to a benefits marketplace to your full-time crew, your part-time workers, or both — without locking into a group plan that penalizes you for a rotating roster.
Do I need 10 or more employees to offer health benefits?
No. The group minimum requirement — typically 5 to 10 enrolled employees — applies to traditional group health plans. It doesn't apply to individual marketplace-based structures. A GC with 3 employees qualifies the same as one with 30.
What does it cost a construction company to offer benefits?
It depends on the structure. Some models allow you to offer access to a benefits marketplace at no monthly cost to you as the employer — employees select and pay for their own plans, with professional guidance included. If you want to contribute toward coverage, most small contractors in construction contribute $100–$300 per enrolled employee per month. You only pay for the people who actually enroll.
Can I offer benefits to 1099 subcontractors?
1099 workers can access individual plans on their own through a benefits marketplace — they're treated as self-employed individuals. You can point them toward the same marketplace you offer employees as a value-add, though employer contribution models are generally structured for W2 employees. A benefits consultant can walk you through the right structure for your specific crew mix.
We already pay a lot for workers' comp. Is there a way to offer health benefits without stacking another huge fixed cost?
Yes — that's exactly the problem individual-based models solve. Instead of a fixed group premium for every employee regardless of enrollment, you pay only for the people who choose to participate. Some employers contribute nothing and simply offer access. Others set a fixed dollar amount per enrolled employee and cap it there. Either way, you're not locked into a monthly bill that grows regardless of who uses the coverage.
