Health Benefits for Cleaning Companies: What's Actually Available in 2026

By Marcus Reid  ·  The Benefits Desk  ·  Sun Mar 15 2026 20:00:00 GMT-0400 (Eastern Daylight Time)

The Workforce Problem Cleaning Business Owners Know Best

Cleaning is one of the most labor-intensive businesses to run. High turnover is a constant. Finding reliable people — the ones who show up, do the work right, and represent your business well to clients — is hard. Keeping them is harder.

Benefits come up in that conversation more than most cleaning business owners expect. Not always explicitly — but it's there. The employee who leaves for the hotel chain. The cleaning lead who takes a job at the office cleaning company because they get a benefits package. The candidate who asks, and the answer has to be no.

The assumption most cleaning operators carry: our workforce is too transient, too part-time, too irregular for benefits to make sense. This isn't our world.

For the traditional group health plan model, that assumption was largely correct. That model wasn't built for a cleaning business.

But a newer model is — and most cleaning companies haven't seen it yet.

Why Traditional Group Insurance Never Fit

Group health insurance requires stability that most cleaning businesses don't have in the way carriers want to see it.

Minimum enrollment requirements. Participation thresholds. Fixed monthly premiums for every eligible employee, whether they want coverage or not. Annual renewals that go up regardless of your crew's actual health usage. A single plan choice that rarely fits the diversity of a cleaning workforce — full-time leads, part-time cleaners, workers with families, workers without, employees spread across income levels.

The model was built for a corporate office. It was never built for a cleaning operation with 8 part-time employees and 3 full-timers.

Most cleaning business owners explored it once, got the quote, and moved on. That's the right call for that model. The mistake is assuming it's the only model.

What's Actually Different Now

A new category of benefits platform has been built specifically for small businesses with workforces that don't fit the traditional mold.

Here's what's different: instead of one plan for everyone, each employee works with a licensed professional — one on one — who reviews their specific situation. Their doctors. Their prescriptions. Their budget. Their family coverage needs. The professional recommends plans that actually fit that specific person. The employee enrolls in what makes sense for them.

The employer doesn't pick the plan. The employer provides access. The employees get real guidance — the kind most of them have never had when navigating health insurance on their own.

For cleaning companies, this model solves two problems at once. You can structure it to cover only your full-time core employees — the ones you're trying to retain. The employer cost can start at zero, or you can contribute a fixed amount per enrolled employee and cap your exposure there. You're never paying for a blanket premium on employees who don't want coverage.

Most cleaning business owners we talk to have never encountered this model. That's changing. And the businesses moving early are building a retention tool most competitors in their market don't have.

Two Options Worth Understanding

Individual marketplace access via a dedicated platform. This is the model built for businesses like yours. A platform installs a benefits marketplace inside your company. Employees who want coverage work one on one with a licensed professional, review their options, and enroll in what fits. You offer real benefits. Your people get real help navigating them — not a website to figure out on their own. The employer cost can be zero.

Self-funded and level-funded plans. For cleaning companies with a more stable full-time crew of 10 or more, self-funded and level-funded plans offer an alternative to traditional group coverage. You pay a fixed monthly amount — typically below standard group plan premiums — and if your crew uses less healthcare than projected, you may get money back. It requires more administrative involvement, but it's worth knowing it exists as your business grows.

For most cleaning operations, the individual marketplace platform is the right starting point. It fits the workforce structure. It fits the budget. It scales with you.

What It Signals to Your Crew

Cleaning work is physically demanding and often undervalued. The employees who do it well and stay consistent are genuinely hard to find and harder to replace.

When a longtime employee asks about benefits and you can say yes — and mean it, with professional guidance included — that changes the relationship. You're not the cleaning company that can't compete with the commercial services firm. You're the employer who actually invested in their people.

That signal moves people. Not just in their decision to stay, but in how they show up. People who feel taken care of tend to act like it.

For a business that runs on reliability and relationships, that's not a soft outcome. That's a real business result.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a cleaning company with mostly part-time workers offer health benefits?
Yes. With individual marketplace access, you can structure benefits for your full-time core employees without being required to extend it to part-time workers. You set the eligibility rules by employee class — and you only pay for the employees who actually enroll.
Do I need a certain number of employees to offer health benefits?
No. The individual marketplace model has no minimum employee count. A cleaning company with 2 full-time employees qualifies the same as one with 20. The group minimum requirement applies only to traditional group health plans.
What does it cost to offer benefits to my cleaning crew?
Some models let you offer access to a professional-guided benefits marketplace at no monthly cost to you as the employer. If you want to contribute, most small cleaning operations start between $100 and $200 per enrolled employee per month. You only pay for employees who enroll — not for everyone on payroll.
My employees don't make a lot of money. Will they be able to afford plans that actually cover something?
That's exactly the problem the individual marketplace model is built to address. Each employee works one on one with a licensed professional who reviews their income, their doctors, their prescriptions, and their family situation before recommending anything. The goal is coverage that actually fits their budget — not a one-size plan that works for some and not others.
What about 1099 cleaning contractors versus W2 employees?
W2 employees can access employer-sponsored individual marketplace benefits. 1099 contractors can access individual plans as self-employed individuals. A benefits professional can walk you through the right structure for your specific workforce mix.
