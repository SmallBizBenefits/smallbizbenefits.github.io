Health Benefits for Roofing Companies: What's Actually Available in 2026

By Marcus Reid  ·  The Benefits Desk  ·  Fri Mar 13 2026 20:00:00 GMT-0400 (Eastern Daylight Time)

The Benefits Gap in the Trades

Roofing is one of the hardest industries to recruit in. The work is physical, the conditions are demanding, and the labor market is tight. Skilled installers have options.

One of the most common reasons roofing contractors lose people to larger companies — and struggle to attract experienced crew members — is benefits. The other company offers health coverage. Yours doesn't. That conversation ends badly more often than most owners want to admit.

The assumption most roofing operators carry: benefits are for companies with 20 or 30 people, and even then, it's expensive and complicated.

That assumption is about ten years out of date.

What the Old Model Looked Like

Traditional group health insurance worked like this: the employer selects a single plan, negotiates with a carrier, and gets a monthly premium for the group. To qualify, you needed a minimum number of employees enrolled — usually 5 to 10 — and most carriers required 70% of eligible employees to participate.

For a roofing crew of 6 to 12, with seasonal workers and a mix of full-time and part-time, this model was nearly impossible to make work. Even when it penciled, the premium was fixed — you paid whether people used the coverage or not — and it went up every year regardless.

That's not the only model anymore.

What Actually Changed

The health insurance market has shifted toward individual coverage. Instead of forcing everyone onto one group plan, employers now have the option to give employees access to a marketplace of individual plans — and employees choose what fits their situation, with help from a licensed professional who walks them through their options.

No single blanket plan. No enrollment minimum. No group negotiation required.

Some structures require no monthly contribution from the employer at all. Others let you contribute a fixed amount per enrolled employee, so you control the cost and only pay for people who actually use it.

For a roofing operator who wants to offer something real without the administrative headache, this is the model worth understanding.

Two Options Worth Knowing

Individual marketplace access. Employees connect with a benefits professional who reviews their doctors, prescriptions, and budget, then walks them through available plans. The employer installs access to the marketplace — in some cases at no monthly cost to the business. Employees handle their own enrollment with professional guidance. You get to say you offer benefits. Your people get real coverage. The admin lift on your end is minimal.

Level-funded plans. For roofing companies with 10 or more employees who want a more structured group-style benefit, level-funded plans are worth looking at. You pay a fixed monthly amount — typically 20–40% less than a traditional fully-insured premium — and if your crew's healthcare usage comes in under projections, you get money back at year end. A healthy, physically active crew in the trades often benefits from this model. It comes with more administrative involvement than the individual marketplace approach, but the economics can work strongly in your favor.

Neither of these is the complicated, expensive group plan most owners picture when they think about benefits.

What It Means for Recruiting and Retention

Benefits aren't just a cost. They're a signal.

When a candidate asks about health coverage and the answer is "we offer it," something shifts. You're no longer the smaller shop that can't compete with the larger contractors. You're the company that takes care of its people.

Contractors who have added a benefits structure in the last few years consistently report two things: better applicant quality and lower turnover. Not because the plans are extravagant — because the signal changed.

At $150 to $200 per enrolled employee per month, you're talking about $1.25 to $1.65 per hour on a full-time basis. For many crew members, that's the deciding factor.

The options are more accessible than most owners realize. The question isn't whether it's possible. It's which structure fits your crew.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a roofing company with seasonal workers offer health benefits?
Yes. The newer individual-based models let you offer benefits without locking into a group plan or a fixed monthly premium for every employee. You can structure it to fit how your crew actually works — including seasonal and part-time workers.
Do I need a minimum number of employees to offer benefits?
No. The old group minimum requirement — typically 5 to 10 enrolled employees — applied to traditional group health plans. It does not apply to individual marketplace-based benefit structures. A crew of two qualifies the same as a crew of twenty.
What does it actually cost a roofing company to offer benefits?
That depends on the structure you choose. Some models allow you to offer access to a benefits marketplace at no monthly cost to you as the employer — employees select and pay for their own plans, with professional guidance. If you want to contribute toward employee premiums, most small contractors contribute $100–$300 per employee per month for the employees who choose to enroll.
Can I offer different benefits to salaried versus hourly crew members?
Yes. You can structure contributions and access differently by employee class — full-time vs. part-time, management vs. field crew. The key is applying the rules consistently within each class.
What's a level-funded plan and is it worth it for a roofing company?
A level-funded plan is a middle ground between traditional fully-insured group coverage and full self-insurance. You pay a fixed monthly amount (lower than standard group premiums), and if your crew uses less care than projected, you can get money back at year end. For a healthy crew in a physically active trade, this can be meaningfully cheaper than buying a standard group plan. It's worth a conversation with a benefits consultant if your team is 10 or more.
