Health Benefits for HVAC Companies: What Small Operations Can Offer in 2026

By Marcus Reid  ·  The Benefits Desk  ·  Sun Mar 15 2026 20:00:00 GMT-0400 (Eastern Daylight Time)

The Technician Shortage Is Real — And Benefits Are Part of the Answer

Every HVAC owner knows the hiring problem. Skilled technicians — the ones who can diagnose a failing compressor, navigate a commercial system, and communicate with a customer — are in short supply. The ones who are good have options. They know it.

When a technician is comparing two offers, health benefits are almost always on the list. The company that offers coverage has an advantage. The company that doesn't has to make it up somewhere else — usually in pay rate, and sometimes it still isn't enough.

The assumption most small HVAC operators carry: we're not big enough to offer benefits. Group plans are for companies with 20 or 30 people.

That assumption is costing people good technicians. And it hasn't been accurate for a while.

Why Group Plans Never Worked for Most HVAC Companies

Traditional group health insurance was built around a workforce model that doesn't fit most small HVAC operations.

To qualify, you needed a minimum number of employees enrolled — typically 5 to 10 — and most carriers required 70% of eligible employees to participate. For an HVAC company with 4 full-time techs and some part-time seasonal help, the math rarely worked. And when it did, you were locked into a fixed monthly premium regardless of whether your crew used the coverage or not. It went up every year. The options were limited. Administration fell on you.

Most small HVAC operators looked at that picture and walked away. That's still the picture most owners think they're facing.

There's a different model now — and most HVAC businesses haven't seen it yet.

A New Kind of Benefits Platform

What's changed is a category of platform built specifically for small businesses that couldn't make the traditional group model work.

Instead of choosing one plan for the whole crew, the employer installs a benefits marketplace inside the business. Each employee works with a licensed professional — one on one — who reviews their specific situation: their doctors, their prescriptions, their budget, their family coverage needs. The professional recommends options that actually fit. The employee picks what works for them.

The employer doesn't choose a plan. The employer provides access. The employees make informed decisions with professional help.

For many HVAC companies, the employer cost can be zero. Some owners offer access as a genuine benefit at no monthly overhead. Others set a fixed contribution per enrolled employee — and only pay for the people who actually enroll. Either way, the structure fits how a small HVAC business actually runs.

Most HVAC owners we talk to have never seen this model before. That's starting to change — and the operators moving early have a recruiting advantage that's hard to replicate.

Two Options Worth Knowing

Individual marketplace access via a dedicated platform. This is the newer model. A platform built for small businesses installs a benefits marketplace inside your company. Each employee connects with a licensed professional, reviews their options, and enrolls in what fits. You offer real benefits. Your people get real guidance. The admin burden on your end is minimal, and the cost can start at zero.

Self-funded and level-funded plans. For HVAC companies with a more stable crew of 10 or more, self-funded and level-funded plans offer an alternative to traditional group coverage. You pay a fixed monthly amount — typically 20 to 40 percent below standard group premiums — and if your crew uses less healthcare than projected, you may get money back at year end. A healthy, active crew in the trades often makes this model attractive. It requires more administrative involvement, but the economics can work strongly in your favor.

The individual marketplace platform is the right starting point for most small HVAC operators. The self-funded conversation becomes more relevant as your crew grows and stabilizes.

What the Numbers Look Like

For an HVAC company owner weighing this decision, here's what the math actually looks like.

If you contribute $200 per enrolled employee per month — a common starting point for small HVAC operators — and you have 5 technicians who enroll, you're looking at $1,000 per month. On a per-hour basis for a full-time tech, that's about $1.15 per hour worked.

That $1.15 per hour difference may be exactly what keeps your best technician from taking a call from the larger HVAC company that offers benefits. It's also what tells a strong candidate — the one you've been trying to recruit — that you're serious about taking care of your people.

If the access-only model makes more sense right now — where employees enroll and pay for their own plans — the cost to you is zero, and you still get to say you offer benefits. A licensed professional still walks each employee through their options. The benefit is real even when the employer contribution is zero.

The options are more accessible than most HVAC owners expect. The question is which structure fits your business right now.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a small HVAC company with 3 to 5 technicians offer health benefits?
Yes. The individual marketplace model doesn't require a minimum number of enrolled employees. An HVAC company with 3 technicians qualifies the same as one with 30. The old group minimum rules apply to traditional group health plans — not to this structure.
What does it cost an HVAC company to offer benefits?
It depends on how you structure it. Some HVAC owners offer access to a benefits marketplace at no monthly cost — employees who want coverage enroll and pay for their own plans with professional guidance included. Others contribute $100 to $300 per enrolled employee per month. You only pay for the employees who actually enroll, not for everyone on payroll.
Can we offer different benefits to office staff versus field technicians?
Yes. You can structure contributions and eligibility differently by employee class — office versus field, full-time versus part-time. The key is applying whatever rules you set consistently within each class.
We're an owner-operator. Can I get coverage for myself through this?
If you have an EIN — which nearly every HVAC business owner does — you can access the individual marketplace as a self-employed business owner. A benefits professional can walk you through what's available for your specific situation.
Is there an alternative to the traditional group plan that could save us money?
Yes. Self-funded and level-funded plans are alternatives to traditional fully-insured group coverage. You pay a fixed monthly amount — typically less than standard group premiums — and if your crew uses less healthcare than projected, you may get money back. For a physically active, healthy HVAC crew, these models can be meaningfully cheaper than what most small operators are currently quoted.
