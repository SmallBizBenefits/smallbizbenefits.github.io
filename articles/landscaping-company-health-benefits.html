Health Benefits for Landscaping Companies: What's Actually Available in 2026

By Marcus Reid  ·  The Benefits Desk  ·  Sun Mar 15 2026 20:00:00 GMT-0400 (Eastern Daylight Time)

The Recruiting Problem Landscaping Owners Know

Finding reliable, skilled crew members in landscaping is harder every year. The work is physical and seasonal, competition for good people is real, and the workers you want — foremen, equipment operators, experienced installers — have options.

One of the most common reasons landscaping companies lose their best people to larger operations comes up in the same conversation every time: benefits. The other company offers health coverage. You don't. That's a harder gap to close than most owners expect.

The assumption most landscaping operators carry: benefits are for bigger companies. We have too many seasonal workers. It's too complicated and too expensive.

That assumption hasn't been accurate for a while. And a new model that most owners in this industry haven't encountered yet makes it more accessible than it's ever been.

Why the Old Model Never Fit Landscaping

Traditional group health insurance was built for a workforce that doesn't exist in landscaping.

To qualify, you needed a minimum number of employees enrolled — usually 5 to 10 — and most carriers required 70% of eligible workers to participate. For a landscaping crew with 8 full-timers in winter and 20 workers in summer, that math never worked cleanly. Seasonal employees skewed the participation numbers. Workers who aged on and off the payroll made administration a headache. And the premium was fixed — you paid the same amount whether your crew used the coverage or not, and it went up every year regardless.

Most landscaping owners looked at that model and walked away. It wasn't built for this business.

What Changed

The shift that most small landscaping operators haven't seen yet: a new category of benefits platform built specifically for businesses like this one.

Instead of forcing a crew onto a single group plan with rigid enrollment rules, these platforms install a benefits marketplace inside your business. Each employee connects with a licensed professional who reviews their specific situation — their doctors, their prescriptions, their budget — and walks them through available plans one on one. The employee picks what fits. The employer sets the structure and the contribution, if any.

No group negotiation. No participation minimum. No single blanket plan that fits some people and works poorly for others.

The employer cost can be zero — some owners simply offer access and let employees who want coverage enroll on their own with professional guidance. Others contribute a fixed amount per enrolled employee and cap it there. Either way, you only pay for the people who actually use it.

Most landscaping business owners we talk to haven't seen this model before. That's changing, but it's still early enough that offering it gives you a real edge in your market.

Two Options Worth Understanding

Individual marketplace access via a dedicated platform. This is the model built for small businesses in industries like landscaping. A platform installs a benefits marketplace inside your operation. Employees who want coverage connect with a licensed professional, walk through their options, and pick a plan that fits. You get to offer benefits. Your people get real guidance — not a website to navigate on their own. The admin lift on your end is minimal, and the employer cost can start at zero.

Self-funded or level-funded plans. For landscaping companies with a larger, more stable year-round crew — 10 or more full-time employees — self-funded and level-funded plans are worth exploring. You pay a fixed monthly amount, typically less than a traditional group plan, and if your crew uses less healthcare than projected, you may get money back at year end. A healthy crew in an outdoor, physically active trade often makes this model attractive. It comes with more administrative involvement, but the economics can work in your favor.

The individual marketplace model is the better starting point for most landscaping operators. The self-funded conversation becomes more relevant as the business grows.

What It Actually Costs

The number that stops most landscaping owners: what does this actually cost me on top of everything else I'm already paying?

At the access-only end, the employer cost is zero. You offer the marketplace. Employees who want coverage enroll and pay for their own plans — with a licensed professional walking them through every option. You've provided a genuine benefit with no monthly overhead added to the business.

If you want to contribute — and many owners do because a contribution is a recruiting signal — most small landscaping operators contribute between $100 and $250 per enrolled employee per month. On a full-time hourly basis, $150/month is roughly 87 cents per hour worked. For the experienced crew member deciding between your company and the next one, that's often what tips it.

You're not paying for the seasonal worker who doesn't enroll. You're not paying for the employee who opts out. That's the structural difference from a traditional group plan — you pay for participation, not eligibility.

What It Means for Your Crew

The landscaping industry runs on relationships. Your foreman who's been with you for six years. The equipment operator who knows every property. The crew lead who trains new hires the right way.

Losing those people to a competitor who offers benefits is expensive — not just in recruiting cost, but in the institutional knowledge that walks out with them.

When you can tell a candidate or a long-term employee that your company offers health benefits and that they'll work with a professional to find the right plan for their situation — something shifts. You're not the small operation that can't compete with the regional landscaping company. You're the employer who actually takes care of people.

That signal matters more in a tight labor market than most owners realize until they've lost someone over it.

What to Do Next

The options are more accessible than most landscaping owners realize. The question is which structure fits how you actually run your business.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a landscaping company with seasonal workers offer health benefits?
Yes. The individual-based model doesn't require year-round, full-time employees or a minimum enrollment count. You can offer access to a benefits marketplace to your core crew without being locked into a group plan that doesn't fit how landscaping actually works — with seasonal swings, mixed hours, and rotating staff.
Do I need a certain number of employees to qualify?
No. The group minimum requirement — typically 5 to 10 enrolled employees — applies to traditional group plans. It doesn't apply to the individual marketplace model. A landscaping company with 2 full-time employees qualifies the same as one with 25.
What does it cost a landscaping company to offer benefits?
Depends on the structure. Some models let you offer access to a professional-guided benefits marketplace at no monthly cost to you as the employer. If you want to contribute toward your crew's coverage, most small landscaping operators land between $100 and $250 per enrolled employee per month — and you only pay for the people who actually enroll.
What about workers who are part-time or seasonal?
You can structure benefits access for your full-time core crew without extending it to seasonal or part-time workers. Contribution levels and eligibility can be set by employee class — full-time versus part-time, year-round versus seasonal. The key is applying the rules consistently within each class.
We already pay a lot in workers' comp and equipment. Is there a way to offer health benefits without a big fixed overhead?
Yes — and that's exactly what the individual-based model was built for. Instead of a fixed group premium whether people use it or not, you pay only for enrolled employees. Some landscaping owners start by offering access at no employer cost, then add a contribution once they see how many employees actually enroll. You control the number.
